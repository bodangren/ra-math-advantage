## CED Learning Objectives

- LO 3.1.A: Identify and describe periodic relationships by examining graphical representations, tables, and equations. Determine the period and interpret the periodic nature of a function in context.

## CED Essential Knowledge

- EK 3.1.A.1: A periodic relationship between two variables occurs when the output values demonstrate a repeating pattern over successive equal-length intervals.
- EK 3.1.A.2: The period of a periodic function is the length of the input values over which the function completes one full cycle. If f is periodic with period P, then f(x) = f(x − P) for all values of x.
- EK 3.1.A.3: Periodic functions can be used to model real-world contexts involving cyclical phenomena such as rotating objects, oscillating motion, and seasonal variation.

## Passwater Scaffolding Notes

Passwater introduces Topic 3.1 with fill-in-the-blank vocabulary definitions: "A **periodic** relationship between two variables occurs when the output values demonstrate a repeating pattern over successive equal-length intervals." Students fill in the blanks for period, interval, and repeating pattern before encountering any symbols.

The conceptual framework establishes that periodic functions can be understood as horizontal translations of themselves: "A periodic function starts with a basic pattern and translates the pattern repeatedly over its domain." The key equation h(x) = h(x − period) is introduced as the defining property students must internalize.

Common misconceptions addressed: (1) Students confuse the interval shown on a graph with the period — the period is the minimum distance for one full cycle, not the width of the visible window. (2) Students struggle with negative inputs — the scaffold shows that h(−2) requires shifting by positive multiples of the period. (3) Students do not recognize composition like h(h(9)) — multiple steps of periodic reasoning are needed.

The scaffolding moves from graph extension (Example 1: "Draw two additional periods"), to reading the period from a graph (Example 2: "What is the least possible value of the period?"), to evaluating at specific inputs (Example 3: g(14), g(72), g(−17)), to table-based evaluation with composition (Example 4: h(h(9)), h(5k − 3)), to inverse reasoning on periodic graphs (Example 5: find all inputs yielding output 1). This builds from concrete visual extension to abstract symbolic reasoning.

## Guided Practice

**Example 1:** "The function f is periodic with period 4. A portion of the graph of f is shown. Draw two additional periods for the graph of f on the axes." — Students copy the visible cycle and translate it right and left by 4 units.

**Example 2:** "A portion of the graph of the periodic function g is shown. What is the least possible value of the period of g?" — Students measure the minimum horizontal distance for a full repeating unit.

**Example 3:** "Using the function g and the period found in Example 2, find: a) g(14) b) g(72) c) g(−17)." — Students subtract multiples of the period to reduce inputs to the known interval.

**Example 4:** "The graph of h is periodic with a period of 5. Values of h are shown in a table: x = 1, 3, 4, 7; h(x) = −2, 0, 3, 2. Find: a) h(−2) b) h(6) c) h(h(9)) d) h(5k − 3)." — For h(−2), shift by 5: h(−2) = h(3) = 0. For h(h(9)), first h(9) = h(4) = 3, then h(3) = 0.

**Example 6 — FRQ 3 Task Model:** "A large clock has an 8-inch-long minute hand. Center is 120 inches above floor. At t = 0, minute hand points directly up. It moves twice as fast, so period is 30 minutes. Part (A): Determine coordinates for five labeled points F, G, J, K, P on the graph of h(t). Part (B): On interval (t₁, t₂), is h positive/negative and increasing/decreasing? Part (B)(ii): Describe how the rate of change of h is changing."

## Independent Practice Description

Worksheet A covers graph-reading problems: find the least period from a graph, evaluate g(9), g(−12), g(29) using periodicity. Worksheet B (FRQ 3 Model): Steve on a seesaw — at t = 0 distance is 24 inches, highest point 60 inches, cycle every 3 seconds. Part (A): determine coordinates for F, G, J, K, P. Part (B): multiple choice on interval behavior and rate of change description. Worksheet C: Prize wheel — center 30 inches from point A, 15 inches from table, 4 rotations per second. Worksheets D/D2/D3: Boat motor contexts with different rotation rates (50, 60, 80 rotations/sec) and distances. Worksheet E: Merry-go-round — counterclockwise, one rotation every 3 seconds, point B is 4 feet from center, center 10 feet from fence. Worksheet F: Ferris wheel — clockwise, one rotation every 30 seconds, B is 40 feet from center, center 50 feet above ground. Worksheet G: "Box O' Fun" toy crank — table 36 inches above ground, crank center 9 inches above table, highest distance 50 inches, one rotation every 4 seconds.

## App-Build Notes

- Recommended componentKey: `fill-in-the-blank`
- Rationale: Periodic vocabulary (period, interval, repeating pattern) is best introduced through fill-in-the-blank definitions before students encounter graphs.
- Calculator requirement: FRQ 3 is NO calculator; practice should be done without calculators.
- Graphing needs: Students must be able to sketch periodic extensions on coordinate axes. Annotate-at-point is needed to read coordinates from periodic graphs.
- Phase package daily phases:
  - Warm-Up: Ask students to list real-world phenomena that repeat (tides, heartbeat, seasons) and describe what "period" means informally.
  - Topic Introduction: Passwater fill-in-the-blank vocabulary — periodic relationship, period, repeating pattern. Introduce h(x) = h(x − period).
  - Scaffolded Examples: Examples 1–3 (extend graph, find period, evaluate g(14)/g(72)/g(−17)).
  - Guided Practice: Example 4 (table with h(h(9)), h(5k − 3)) with teacher prompting.
  - Independent Practice: Worksheet A Problems 1–6 (graph reading and evaluation).
  - Exit Evidence: Given f is periodic with period 6 and f(2) = 5, find f(14).
  - CAP Reflection: "What does it mean for a function to repeat every P units? How do you find the output when the input is far from the visible interval?"

## Additional Notes

The FRQ 3 task model is first introduced here and recurs throughout Topics 3.4–3.7. The five-point coordinate structure (F, G, J, K, P) maps to one full period of a sinusoidal graph with F and P at the same value (start/end of cycle), G at a quarter-period, J at the midline crossing, and K at three-quarters. Students should practice labeling these points on any periodic context.

Passwater uses physical contexts that appear repeatedly across worksheets: the clock with the 8-inch hand, the seesaw with Steve, the prize wheel, boat motors with varying rotation rates, the merry-go-round, the Ferris wheel, and the toy crank. These same contexts appear with increasing mathematical complexity in Topics 3.4, 3.5, and 3.7.

The quiz after Topic 3.1 tests graph extension, period identification, periodic evaluation from graphs and tables, and the FRQ 3 task model with coordinates and interval behavior.
