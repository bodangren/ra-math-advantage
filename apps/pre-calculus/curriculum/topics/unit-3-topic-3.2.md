## CED Learning Objectives

- LO 3.2.A: Define and compute sine, cosine, and tangent of angles in standard position using coordinates on circles. Convert between degree and radian measures and interpret angles on the unit circle.

## CED Essential Knowledge

- EK 3.2.A.1: An angle in standard position has its vertex at the origin and one ray on the positive x-axis. The terminal ray is the second ray. sin θ = y/r, cos θ = x/r, and tan θ = y/x where (x, y) is on the terminal ray and r is the distance from the origin.
- EK 3.2.A.2: The radian measure of an angle is the ratio of the arc length to the radius. On the unit circle (r = 1), sin θ = y, cos θ = x, and tan θ = y/x.
- EK 3.2.A.3: Positive angles are measured counterclockwise; negative angles are measured clockwise. The unit circle is a circle centered at the origin with radius 1.

## Passwater Scaffolding Notes

Passwater splits Topic 3.2 into two parts. Part I (Sine, Cosine, Tangent) introduces formal definitions using "terminal ray" language consistently. The hook states: "In this course, we are going to approach angles in a way that may be different than what you have seen in the past." Definitions: sin θ is the ratio of vertical displacement to r; cos θ is the ratio of horizontal displacement to r; tan θ is the slope of the terminal ray (y/x or sin θ / cos θ).

Part II (Radian Angle Measures) introduces radian measure as arc length / radius. Key note: "For a unit circle, the radian measure is the same as the arc length formed by the angle." The unit circle is emphasized: "The concept of the unit circle will become pivotal throughout the rest of this course because of its incredible usefulness when working with trigonometric values." When r = 1, simplifications apply: sin θ = y, cos θ = x.

Scaffolding builds from definitions → coordinate representations on circles of various radii → reflections of points P, Q, R, S across axes → radian measure conversion → unit circle quadrant I values. Vocabulary is never assumed — standard position, terminal ray, radian, and unit circle are all fill-in-the-blank.

## Guided Practice

**Part I Example 1:** Circle radius 3. Multiple choice: Which statement is true about cos θ? (Options involve cos θ = x/3, cos θ = y/3, etc.)

**Part I Example 2:** Circle radius 5, point P. Fill in: sin θ = ___, cos θ = ___, tan θ = ___.

**Part I Example 3:** Circle with point P(x, y). Q, R, S are reflections of P across y-axis, origin, x-axis. Find coordinates of Q = (−x, y), R = (−x, −y), S = (x, −y).

**Part I Example 7:** "Let θ be an angle whose terminal ray coincides with y = −3x in quadrant II. Find sin θ, cos θ, tan θ." — Choose P = (−1, 3), r = √10. sin θ = 3/√10, cos θ = −1/√10, tan θ = −3.

**Part II Example 3:** Find radian measures: a) 1/6 of unit circle counterclockwise → π/3; b) 2 full revolutions clockwise → −4π; c) 3/4 counterclockwise → 3π/2; d) 7/8 counterclockwise → 7π/4.

**Part II Example 5:** P(1/2, √3/2). sin θ = √3/2, cos θ = 1/2, tan θ = √3.

## Additional Notes

The "terminal ray" language used by Passwater is deliberate — it avoids degree-first conventions and primes students for radian measure. When reflecting points P across axes to get Q, R, S, students build the symmetry pattern that makes unit circle evaluation efficient: Q = (−x, y) for y-axis reflection, R = (−x, −y) for origin reflection, S = (x, −y) for x-axis reflection. These coordinate transformations underlie all quadrant sign rules for sin, cos, and tan.

Part II's 36-problem worksheet emphasizes angle sketching in standard position and matching figures to radian measures. This fluency is prerequisite for Topics 3.3–3.12 where students evaluate trig functions at standard angles.
