## CED Learning Objectives

- LO 3.5.A: Define sinusoidal functions as additive and multiplicative transformations of sine and cosine. Identify sinusoidal functions and connect them to periodic contexts.

## CED Essential Knowledge

- EK 3.5.A.1: A sinusoidal function is any function that involves additive and multiplicative transformations of f(θ) = sin θ.
- EK 3.5.A.2: The cosine function is a sinusoidal function because g(θ) = cos θ = sin(θ + π/2).
- EK 3.5.A.3: Sinusoidal functions model periodic phenomena with midline, amplitude, period, and frequency as defining characteristics.

## Passwater Scaffolding Notes

Passwater treats Topics 3.4 and 3.5 as a combined instructional block. The key definitional moment: "A **sinusoidal function** is any function that involves additive and multiplicative transformations of f(θ) = sin θ." The bridge from cos to sin is explicit: "The sine and cosine functions are both sinusoidal functions because g(θ) = cos θ = sin(θ + π/2)."

This topic focuses on the identification question: given a graph or description, determine if the function is sinusoidal, and extract its four defining parameters. The scaffolding connects to Topic 3.1 (periodic phenomena) and Topic 3.4 (graph properties) by asking students to synthesize both ideas — a sinusoidal function is a periodic function whose period, amplitude, midline, and frequency can be explicitly described.

Passwater emphasizes describing behavior over sub-intervals: increasing at an increasing rate, increasing at a decreasing rate, decreasing at an increasing rate, decreasing at a decreasing rate. This language prepares students for FRQ 3 Part (B) responses.

## Guided Practice

**Example 1:** Graph of f(θ). Find period, frequency, amplitude, midline. Students read max/min values and horizontal span from the graph.

**Example 2:** Graph of g(θ). Same task — students identify the four parameters.

**Example 3:** h(θ) has max at (π, 8), min at (3π, −2). Students compute:
- Midline = (8 + (−2))/2 = 3
- Amplitude = (8 − (−2))/2 = 5
- Period = 2 × (3π − π) = 4π
- Frequency = 1/(4π)

**Example 4 — FRQ 3 Revisited:** Clock context (8-inch minute hand, 120 inches above floor, 30-minute period). Part (A): coordinates for F, G, J, K, P. Part (B): period, frequency, amplitude, midline. Part (C): two intervals where h is decreasing and concave up.

**Behavior Analysis:** For a sinusoidal graph, describe over (0 < θ < π/2): choices include increasing/decreasing at increasing/decreasing rate; concave up/down; positive/negative and increasing/decreasing.

## Additional Notes

The distinction between periodic and sinusoidal is critical. A square wave or sawtooth wave is periodic but not sinusoidal. Only functions that are transformations of sin or cos qualify as sinusoidal. This distinction matters for modeling — sinusoidal models assume smooth oscillation, which is appropriate for tides and temperatures but not for discrete on/off cycles.
