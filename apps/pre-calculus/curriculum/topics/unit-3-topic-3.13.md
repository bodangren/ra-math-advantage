## CED Learning Objectives

- LO 3.13.A: Convert between polar and rectangular coordinates. Represent complex numbers in polar form. Plot points in the polar coordinate system.

## CED Essential Knowledge

- EK 3.13.A.1: In polar coordinates, a point A is defined by (r, θ) where r is the radius and θ is the angle in standard position whose terminal ray includes A. The same point can be represented in multiple ways.
- EK 3.13.A.2: Polar to rectangular: x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ. Rectangular to polar: r² = x² + y², tan θ = y/x.
- EK 3.13.A.3: Complex numbers in rectangular form a + bi correspond to the point (a, b). In polar form, the complex number is r(cos θ + i sin θ) where (r, θ) are polar coordinates.

## Passwater Scaffolding Notes

Passwater introduces polar coordinates with the definition: "In polar coordinates, the point A is defined by (r, θ) where r represents the radius of the circle on which point A lies, and θ represents the measure of an angle in standard position whose terminal ray includes point A." The key insight: "One really cool result of using polar coordinates is that the same point can be represented many different ways!"

Conversion formulas are the core skill:
- Polar to rectangular: x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ (from Topics 3.2–3.3)
- Rectangular to polar: r² = x² + y² (Pythagorean theorem), tan θ = y/x

The tip is important: "Whenever you are converting coordinates from one system to another, always sketch out the point first and check that your answer makes sense!" This addresses a common error — students forget to check the quadrant when computing θ from tan θ = y/x.

Complex numbers connect algebra to trigonometry: rectangular form a + bi → point (a, b) → polar form r(cos θ + i sin θ). Passwater gives: "The complex number 3 + 2i can be represented as the point (3, 2) in the rectangular coordinate system."

Scaffolding moves: plot polar coordinates (Examples 1–2) → multiple representations of same point (Example 3) → polar to rectangular conversion (Example 4) → rectangular to polar conversion (Example 5) → complex number rectangular ↔ polar (Examples 6–7).

## Guided Practice

**Example 1:** Plot A(2, π/3), B(3, π/2), C(4, π), D(2, −π/6) on polar grid.

**Example 2:** Plot A(2, π/4), B(7, −π/2), C(9, π/2), D(5, −π/2) — shows same point can be represented with different r and θ.

**Example 3:** Plot (5, π/2). Write two different polar representations: (5, π/2) and (5, 5π/2) or (−5, 3π/2).

**Example 4:** Convert polar to rectangular: a) (2, π) → (−2, 0), b) (5, 4π/3) → (5 cos(4π/3), 5 sin(4π/3)) = (−5/2, −5√3/2), c) (7, −π/2) → (0, −7).

**Example 5:** Convert rectangular to polar: a) (2, −2) → r = √8 = 2√2, θ = −π/4 (or 7π/4). b) (−1, √3) → r = 2, θ = 2π/3. c) (0, −5) → r = 5, θ = 3π/2.

**Example 6:** Complex number with rectangular coordinates (−2, −2). r = √8 = 2√2, θ = 5π/4. Polar form: 2√2(cos(5π/4) + i sin(5π/4)).

**Example 7:** Polar: 7(cos(7π/6) + i sin(7π/6)). Rectangular: 7(−√3/2) + 7(−1/2)i = −7√3/2 − 7i/2.
