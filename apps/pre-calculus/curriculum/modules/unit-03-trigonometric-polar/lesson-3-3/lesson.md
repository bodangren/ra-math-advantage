# Lesson 3-3 — Sine and Cosine Function Values

Unit: 3
Topic: 3.3
AP Exam: assessed-on-ap-exam

## Explore

### Section: text

An equilateral triangle with side length 1 is inscribed in the unit circle, with one vertex on the positive x-axis. The angle at the origin for one of the other vertices is [\pi/6]. Can you find the exact coordinates of that point on the unit circle? What about the point at [\pi/4]?

These geometric constructions give us exact sine and cosine values at key angles — values you will use throughout the entire trigonometry unit.

## Vocabulary

### Section: text

- **Special angles**: Angles that are multiples of [\pi/6], [\pi/4], [\pi/3], and [\pi/2] — they have exact sine and cosine values.
- **Pythagorean identity**: The equation [\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1], which holds for every angle [\theta] and follows from the Pythagorean theorem on the unit circle.
- **Reference angle**: The acute angle between the terminal ray and the x-axis, used to find trig values in any quadrant.

On the unit circle, [\cos \theta] and [\sin \theta] are the [x]- and [y]-coordinates of the point at angle [\theta]. The coordinates of any point on a circle of radius [r] are [(r\cos \theta, r\sin \theta)].

## Learn

### Section: text

The coordinates of a point on a circle of radius [r] can be written as [(r\cos \theta, r\sin \theta)]. On the unit circle ([r = 1]), [\cos \theta] is the [x]-coordinate and [\sin \theta] is the [y]-coordinate.

**Geometric derivation of special values:**

For [\theta = \pi/6]: An equilateral triangle inscribed in the unit circle has a vertex at height [y = 1/2]. By the Pythagorean theorem, [x = \sqrt{1 - (1/2)^2} = \sqrt{3}/2]. So [\cos(\pi/6) = \sqrt{3}/2] and [\sin(\pi/6) = 1/2].

For [\theta = \pi/4]: An isosceles right triangle gives [x = y] and [x^2 + y^2 = 1], so [2x^2 = 1] and [x = y = \sqrt{2}/2].

For [\theta = \pi/3]: By symmetry, [\cos(\pi/3) = 1/2] and [\sin(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3}/2].

These three sets of values in quadrant I are the foundation. All other unit circle values are obtained by reflecting across axes — changing signs as appropriate for each quadrant.

\[\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 \quad \text{for all } \theta\]

## Worked Example

### Section: text

**Example 1:** Find the exact value of [\sin(2\pi/3)].

**Solution:** The angle [2\pi/3] is in quadrant II. Its reference angle is [\pi - 2\pi/3 = \pi/3]. In quadrant II, sine is positive. [\sin(2\pi/3) = \sin(\pi/3) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}]

**Example 2:** Find the exact value of [\cos(5\pi/4)].

**Solution:** The angle [5\pi/4] is in quadrant III. Reference angle: [5\pi/4 - \pi = \pi/4]. In quadrant III, cosine is negative. [\cos(5\pi/4) = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}]

**Example 3:** Find [\sin(7\pi/6)], [\cos(11\pi/6)], and [\sin(3\pi/2)].

**Solution:**
- [\sin(7\pi/6)]: Quadrant III, reference angle [\pi/6]. Sine is negative. Result: [-\frac{1}{2}]
- [\cos(11\pi/6)]: Quadrant IV, reference angle [\pi/6]. Cosine is positive. Result: [\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}]
- [\sin(3\pi/2)]: On the negative [y]-axis. Result: [-1]

**Example 4:** The point [P] is on a circle of radius 4 with an equilateral triangle. If the angle at [P] is [2\pi/3], find the coordinates of [P].

**Solution:** [P = (4\cos(2\pi/3), 4\sin(2\pi/3)) = (4 \cdot (-1/2), 4 \cdot (\sqrt{3}/2)) = (-2, 2\sqrt{3})]

## Guided Practice

### Section: text

**Problem 1:** Find the exact values of [\sin(5\pi/6)] and [\cos(5\pi/6)].

**Hint:** [5\pi/6] is in quadrant II with reference angle [\pi/6]. In quadrant II, sine is positive and cosine is negative.

**Problem 2:** Find the exact value of [\cos(4\pi/3)].

**Hint:** [4\pi/3] is in quadrant III with reference angle [\pi/3]. Cosine is negative in quadrant III.

**Problem 3:** If [\cos \theta = 3/5] and [\theta] is in quadrant IV, find [\sin \theta] using the Pythagorean identity.

**Hint:** [\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta = 1 - 9/25 = 16/25]. In quadrant IV, sine is negative.

## Reflection

### Section: text

**Exit Ticket:** Find the exact values of [\sin(5\pi/3)], [\cos(2\pi/3)], and [\sin(7\pi/6)].

**Lesson Summary:** Exact sine and cosine values at special angles come from geometric constructions — equilateral triangles for [\pi/6] and [\pi/3], isosceles right triangles for [\pi/4]. The three quadrant I coordinate pairs [(\sqrt{3}/2, 1/2)], [(\sqrt{2}/2, \sqrt{2}/2)], and [(1/2, \sqrt{3}/2)] are the foundation. All other values are found by reflecting into the correct quadrant and adjusting signs. The Pythagorean identity [\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1] connects sine and cosine for every angle.
