## Explore

### Section: text

Plot [\sin \theta] for [\theta] from [0] to [2\pi] using the unit circle values you know: [\sin 0 = 0], [\sin(\pi/6) = 1/2], [\sin(\pi/2) = 1], [\sin(\pi) = 0], [\sin(3\pi/2) = -1], [\sin(2\pi) = 0]. Connect the dots smoothly.

What shape emerges? How high and low does it go? How far along the [θ]-axis does one full pattern take? These questions define the key features of every sinusoidal graph.

## Vocabulary

### Section: text

- **Midline**: The horizontal line halfway between the maximum and minimum values of a sinusoidal function. If the maximum is [M] and the minimum is [m], the midline is [y = (M + m)/2].
- **Amplitude**: The distance from the midline to the maximum (or minimum). Amplitude = [(M - m)/2].
- **Period**: The horizontal length of one full cycle.
- **Frequency**: The reciprocal of the period — the number of cycles per unit of input. Frequency = [1/P].
- **Sinusoidal function**: A function that can be expressed as a transformation of [\sin \theta] or [\cos \theta].

## Learn

### Section: text

The graphs of [f(\theta) = \sin \theta] and [g(\theta) = \cos \theta] share the same basic properties:

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Midline | [y = 0] |
| Amplitude | [1] |
| Period | [2\pi] |
| Frequency | [1/(2\pi)] |

The sine graph starts at the origin, rises to [1] at [\pi/2], crosses zero at [\pi], falls to [-1] at [3\pi/2], and returns to [0] at [2\pi]. The cosine graph starts at [1], crosses zero at [\pi/2], reaches [-1] at [\pi], crosses zero again at [3\pi/2], and returns to [1] at [2\pi].

The graph of [\sin \theta] oscillates between **concave down** (above the midline) and **concave up** (below the midline). This concavity pattern is essential for describing interval behavior.

The cosine function is a horizontal shift of sine: [\cos \theta = \sin(\theta + \pi/2)].

## Worked Example

### Section: text

**Example 1:** A sinusoidal function [h(\theta)] has a maximum value of [8] and a minimum value of [-2]. Find the midline, amplitude, period (if max occurs at [\pi] and next min at [3\pi]), and frequency.

**Solution:**
- Midline: [y = (8 + (-2))/2 = 3]
- Amplitude: [(8 - (-2))/2 = 5]
- Period: [2(3\pi - \pi) = 4\pi]
- Frequency: [1/(4\pi)]

**Example 2:** Describe the behavior of [f(\theta) = \sin \theta] on the interval [(0, \pi/2)].

**Solution:** On [(0, \pi/2)], [f] is **increasing** and **concave down** (the graph is above the midline and bending toward the maximum).

**Example 3:** Describe the behavior on [(\pi/2, \pi)].

**Solution:** On [(\pi/2, \pi)], [f] is **decreasing** and **concave down** (above the midline, curving away from the peak).

## Guided Practice

### Section: text

**Problem 1:** A sinusoidal graph has a maximum of [12] at [\theta = 0] and the next minimum of [4] at [\theta = \pi]. Find the midline, amplitude, period, and frequency.

**Hint:** The midline is the average of max and min. The period is twice the horizontal distance from max to min.

**Problem 2:** On the interval [(\pi, 3\pi/2)], is [\sin \theta] increasing or decreasing? Is it concave up or concave down?

**Hint:** Sine is below the midline on [(\pi, 2\pi)] and heading toward its minimum.

**Problem 3:** A sinusoidal function [g] has a minimum at [(\pi/2, 0)] and a maximum at [(\pi, 1)]. Find the midline and amplitude.

**Hint:** Midline = [(1 + 0)/2 = 0.5]. Amplitude = [(1 - 0)/2 = 0.5].

## Reflection

### Section: text

**Exit Ticket:** A sinusoidal function has a maximum at [(2, 10)] and the next minimum at [(6, 2)]. Find the amplitude and midline.

**Lesson Summary:** The graphs of sine and cosine share the same fundamental shape — smooth, repeating waves. Four parameters define any sinusoidal graph: the midline (vertical center), amplitude (distance from midline to peak), period (horizontal length of one cycle), and frequency (cycles per unit). A sinusoidal graph is concave down above the midline and concave up below it, which determines how to describe behavior on sub-intervals.
