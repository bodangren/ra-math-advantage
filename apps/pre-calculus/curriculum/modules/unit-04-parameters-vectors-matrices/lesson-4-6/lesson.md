# Lesson 4-6 — Conic Sections

Unit: 4
Topic: 4.6
AP Exam: not-assessed-on-ap-exam

## Explore

### Section: text

Slice a cone with a flat plane. Depending on the angle, you get a circle, ellipse, parabola, or hyperbola. These four curves — **conic sections** — arise in orbits, optics, architecture, and navigation. Each has a standard implicit equation, and each reveals different geometric properties.

## Vocabulary

### Section: text

- **Conic section**: A curve formed by the intersection of a plane with a double cone.
- **Circle**: [(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2] — center [(h, k)], radius [r].
- **Ellipse**: [frac{(x - h)^2}{a^2} + frac{(y - k)^2}{b^2} = 1] — center [(h, k)], semi-axes [a] and [b].
- **Parabola**: [y = a(x - h)^2 + k] (vertical) — vertex [(h, k)].
- **Hyperbola**: [frac{(x - h)^2}{a^2} - frac{(y - k)^2}{b^2} = 1] — center [(h, k)], opens left-right.
- **Standard form**: The canonical equation that reveals the conic's center, axes, and shape.

## Learn

### Section: text

The four conic sections, in standard form centered at the origin:

| Conic | Standard Form | Key Feature |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| Circle | [x^2 + y^2 = r^2] | Equal coefficients for [x^2] and [y^2] |
| Ellipse | [frac{x^2}{a^2} + frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1] | Both terms positive, different denominators |
| Parabola | [y = ax^2] (or [x = ay^2]) | Only one squared term |
| Hyperbola | [frac{x^2}{a^2} - frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1] | Subtraction between squared terms |

**Identifying from a general equation** [Ax^2 + By^2 + Cx + Dy + E = 0]:
- [A = B neq 0]: circle
- [A neq B], same sign: ellipse
- One of [A, B] is 0: parabola
- [A] and [B] have opposite signs: hyperbola

**Graphing strategy**: Rewrite in standard form by completing the square, then identify center, axes, and vertices.

## Worked Example

### Section: text

**Example 1:** Identify and graph [4x^2 + 9y^2 = 36].

**Solution:** Divide by 36:
\[ frac{x^2}{9} + frac{y^2}{4} = 1 \]
This is an **ellipse** centered at the origin with [a = 3] (x-intercepts at [pm 3]) and [b = 2] (y-intercepts at [pm 2]).

**Example 2:** Identify [x^2 - 4y^2 + 6x + 8y + 5 = 0].

**Solution:** Completing the square gives [(x + 3)^2 - 4(y - 1)^2 = 0], a **degenerate hyperbola** (two intersecting lines: [x + 3 = pm 2(y - 1)]).

## Guided Practice

### Section: text

**Problem 1:** Classify [9x^2 + 16y^2 = 144] as a conic section and find its intercepts.

**Hint:** Divide by 144 to get standard form.

**Problem 2:** Classify [x^2 + y^2 - 4x + 6y - 12 = 0] and find the center and radius.

**Hint:** Complete the square for both [x] and [y].

## Independent Practice

### Section: text

**Problem 1:** Write the equation [frac{x^2}{25} + frac{y^2}{16} = 1]. Identify the conic and find all intercepts.

**Problem 2:** Classify [y^2 - 9x^2 = 81]. What type of conic is this?

**Problem 3:** Complete the square to convert [x^2 + y^2 + 8x - 10y + 5 = 0] to standard form. Identify the center and radius.

## Reflection

### Section: text

**Exit Ticket:** Classify [25x^2 + 4y^2 = 100] as a conic section and state its center.

**Lesson Summary:** Conic sections are classified by their standard forms: circles (equal squared coefficients), ellipses (addition, different denominators), parabolas (one squared term), and hyperbolas (subtraction). Completing the square converts general equations to standard form.
