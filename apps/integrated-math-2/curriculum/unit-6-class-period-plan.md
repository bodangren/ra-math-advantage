# Unit 6 Class-Period Plan

This document is the audited class-period breakdown for Unit 6 (Measurement).

It should be treated as the canonical planning source for Unit 6 class periods unless a later audit supersedes it.

It follows the planning model in `curriculum/course-spec.md`:

- the **class period** is the atomic planning unit
- `instruction` days must map to `measure/course-objectives.md`
- `instruction` days must name the specific worked examples they use
- `mastery`, `jigsaw`, `review`, and `test` days are marked primarily by day type

## Notes

- This is a **planning model**, not a literal calendar.
- Most instruction days use **2-3 worked examples**.
- A few objective-isolating days use **1 anchor example** plus guided practice, discussion, or error analysis.
- Some course objectives are treated as **embedded objectives** rather than the single primary objective for a day.
- With 5 textbook lessons and 12 instruction days, lessons receive the following day counts: 6-1 × 2, 6-2 × 2, 6-3 × 2, 6-4 × 3, 6-5 × 3.
- The unit includes a mid-unit review after surface area, providing a consolidation point before volume and cross-sections.

## Why This Split Works

- `6-1` splits into a standard-area-formulas day (triangle, parallelogram, trapezoid) and an apothem / composite / real-world day.
- `6-2` splits into a prism-and-pyramid surface area day and a cone-and-sphere surface area day.
- `6-3` splits into a core volume formulas day (rectangular prism, triangular prism, cylinder) and a comparison / real-world day.
- `6-4` splits into a cone-and-sphere volume day, a comparison / hemisphere day, and a real-world / composite-solid day.
- `6-5` splits into a core cross-section identification day (cube, cylinder), a composite-solid-volume day, and a Cavalieri's-principle / real-world day.
- The mid-unit review after surface area gives students a consolidation point before transitioning to volume and cross-sections.
- The non-instruction days create room for mastery cycles and mathematical-practices work across the full range of area, surface area, and volume formulas.
