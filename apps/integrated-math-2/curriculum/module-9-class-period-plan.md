# Module 9 Class-Period Plan

This document is the audited class-period breakdown for Module 9.

It should be treated as the canonical planning source for Module 9 class periods unless a later audit supersedes it.

It follows the planning model in `curriculum/course-spec.md`:

- the **class period** is the atomic planning unit
- `instruction` days must map to `measure/course-objectives.md`
- `instruction` days must name the specific worked examples they use
- `mastery`, `jigsaw`, `review`, and `test` days are marked primarily by day type

## Notes

- This is a **planning model**, not a literal calendar.
- Most instruction days use **2-3 worked examples**.
- A few objective-isolating days use **1 anchor example** plus guided practice, discussion, or error analysis.
- Some course objectives are treated as **embedded objectives** rather than the single primary objective for a day.
- Module 9 is relatively lean at 5 lessons; the two-instruction-days-per-lesson structure gives sufficient depth for the multi-step skills involved.
- Linear programming applications naturally fit the jigsaw day structure.

## Why This Split Works

- `9-1` is foundational for all subsequent algebra; giving it two days — one for procedure, one for special cases — builds both fluency and recognition.
- `9-2` (graphing linear equations) and `9-3` (inequalities) each get two instruction days, which matches the representational complexity of converting between forms and graphing shaded regions.
- The jigsaw after `9-3` provides a natural break between equations/inequalities and systems, connecting the module's multi-step skills to real-world modeling.
- `9-4` (systems) and `9-5` (systems of inequalities) are conceptually linked; the mastery day between them consolidates systems-of-equations methods before introducing the inequalities extension.
