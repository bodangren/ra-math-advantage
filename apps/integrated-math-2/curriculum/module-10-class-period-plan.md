# Module 10 Class-Period Plan

This document is the audited class-period breakdown for Module 10.

It should be treated as the canonical planning source for Module 10 class periods unless a later audit supersedes it.

It follows the planning model in `curriculum/course-spec.md`:

- the **class period** is the atomic planning unit
- `instruction` days must map to `measure/course-objectives.md`
- `instruction` days must name the specific worked examples they use
- `mastery`, `jigsaw`, `review`, and `test` days are marked primarily by day type

## Notes

- This is a **planning model**, not a literal calendar.
- Most instruction days use **2-3 worked examples**.
- A few objective-isolating days use **1 anchor example** plus guided practice, discussion, or error analysis.
- Some course objectives are treated as **embedded objectives** rather than the single primary objective for a day.
- Module 10 has strong vertical connections: laws of exponents build to negative/rational exponents, which connect to radicals, which are used in solving radical equations.
- The jigsaw day bridges from exponent rules to scientific notation and radical expressions.

## Why This Split Works

- `10-1` (laws of exponents) is the load-bearing skill for the rest of the module; giving it two days ensures students have full fluency before negative and rational exponents are introduced.
- `10-2` bridges exponents and radicals; the two-day split separates the negative-exponent concept from the rational-exponent-to-radical conversion, which are often conflated by students.
- The jigsaw after `10-3` creates deliberate space between scientific notation (base-10) and the radical expressions content that follows (general roots), reinforcing that these are related but distinct representations.
- `10-4` (radical expressions) and `10-5` (solving radical equations) are tightly coupled; the mastery day between them consolidates manipulation skills before the solving-with-checking complexity is added.
